</ul><h3>Top Three Books of 2021</h3>

<p><strong><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/What%27s_Bred_in_the_Bone">What’s Bred in the Bone</a> - Robertson Davies</strong></p>

<blockquote>
  <p>“A happy childhood has spoiled many a promising life.”</p>
</blockquote>

<p>This was my only re-read of 2021. I think this is my favourite Davies novel, but I tend to think that after reading any of his books. It’s part two of his Cornish Trilogy, and shares some characters and settings from his Deptford Trilogy. Who was Franis Cornish? I’m a sucker for coming-of-age stories, but mix in art, philosophy, religion, spies!, forgery, provincial Canadiana, angles and daimons, and you know I’ll be hooked throughout.</p>

<blockquote>
  <p>“The art of the quoter is to know when to stop.”</p>
</blockquote>

<p><strong><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sapiens:_A_Brief_History_of_Humankind">Sapiens - A Brief History of Humankind</a> by Yuval Noah Harari</strong></p>

<blockquote>
  <p>“You could never convince a monkey to give you a banana by promising him limitless bananas after death in monkey heaven.”</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Although I disagreed with some of the book’s claims, they are presented with such conviction that I just went along for the ride; it’s a good ride if slightly depressing. Harari’s history of humanity begins with the evolution of imagination and concludes with an exploration of human happiness. A 100,000 year story of how we (homo sapiens) outlived five other human species and (for better or worse) came to dominate the world.</p>

<blockquote>
  <p>“Biology enables, Culture forbids.”</p>
</blockquote>

<p><strong><a href="https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/41739506-math-art">Math Art: Truth, Beauty, and Equations</a> by Stephen Ornes</strong></p>

<p>A coffee table art book with artist interviews and high-level explorations of aesthetically pleasing mathematical concepts. Goes beyond the usual math art of Escher and Fractals, although both are mentioned. If you’re intrigued, the book is available from the Winnipeg Public Library.</p>

<p>Instead of a quote I’ve included photo from the book at the bottom of this post.</p>
