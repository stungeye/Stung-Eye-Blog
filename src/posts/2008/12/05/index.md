## Pirates of the Amazon Abandon Ship

<p>Dutch students “release <a href="http://pirates-of-the-amazon.com/">a Firefox extension</a> that made it easy for people who were browsing books, music and movies on <a href="http://amazon.com">Amazon</a> to download the same products free through <a href="http://thepiratebay.org/">the Pirate Bay</a>, the illicit <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BitTorrent">BitTorrent</a> site.”</p>

<p>Amazon serves a take-down notice. Students comply.</p>

<p>Student defend their work as parody:</p>

<p>“[Amazon and the Pirate Bay] might look like opposites, but are actually quite similar in regards to the mainstream media content they provide. Our project demonstrated this practically. So it’s a parody of any kind of media consumerism, whether corporate or subcultural.”</p>

<p>The students weren’t hosting or providing pirated media. They <em>were</em> pointing the way. Will this eventually lead to world governments enacting legislation that limits certain types of linking on the net? Might it eventually be illegal for me or <a href="http://www.google.ca/search?q=bittorrent">Google</a> to <a href="http://thepiratebay.org/">link to the Pirate Bay</a>?</p>
