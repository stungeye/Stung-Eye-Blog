<a href="http://www.stevenberlinjohnson.com/" target="_blank">Steven Johnson</a>, pop-science author, majored in <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semiotics" target="_blank">Semiotics</a> as a <a href="http://www.brown.edu" target="_blank">Brown</a> undergrad. <a href="http://www.stevenberlinjohnson.com/movabletype/archives/000168.html" target="_blank">His latest blog entry</a> deals with this "theoretical education" and lead me to <a href="http://www.boston.com/news/globe/ideas/articles/2004/05/16/the_semio_grads/" target="_blank">Paul Greenberg's article on the Brown Semiotics major</a>.

Greenberg states that "semiotics is about how we derive meaning from context." He goes on to explain the Ferdinand de Saussure, who coined the term, "posited that no word is inherently meaningful. Rather a word is only a 'signifier', i.e. the representation of something, and it must be combined in the brain with the 'signified', or the thing itself, in order to form a meaning-imbued 'sign'."

Having often pondered the role of context, analogy and symbolism in <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Memetics" target="_blank">Memetics</a>, Semiotics could prove to be a fascinating tool.
