## Reading and Listening in 2016

<p>I read 17 books last year. Two more than <a href="http://stungeye.com/archive/by_date/2016/02/19">2015</a>. Eight less than <a href="http://stungeye.com/archive/by_date/2015/01/22">2014</a>, Two less than in <a href="http://stungeye.com/archive/by_date/2014/01/11">2013</a>, one less than in <a href="http://stungeye.com/archive/by_date/2012/12/31">2012</a>, and one more than in <a href="http://stungeye.com/archive/by_date/2011/12/18">2011</a>. All seventeen books were deadtree format. Thirteen of them were fiction. Four were non-fiction.</p>

<p>If we also count the books I’ve read to my girls before bed, the number would larger. This was the first year I started reading chapter books with the girls. We finished four chapter books together.</p>

<p>As in 2015, I listened to a large number of podcasts, but I took a break from audio books and audio lectures.</p>

<h3>Fiction Read in 2016</h3>

<ul><li><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harry_Potter_and_the_Order_of_the_Phoenix">Harry Potter and the Order of the Phoenix</a> - J. K. Rowling</li>
<li><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Q_%28novel%29">Q</a> - Luther Blissett - 16th century Europe. Reformation, early capitalism, and the journey of an Anabaptist radical.</li>
<li><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harry_Potter_and_the_Half-Blood_Prince">Harry Potter and the Half-Blood Prince</a> - J. K. Rowling</li>
<li><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/How_to_Live_Safely_in_a_Science_Fictional_Universe">How to Live Safely in a Science Fictional Universe</a> - Charles Yu - An exploration of the melancholy nature of consciousness. And time-travel.</li>
<li><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harry_Potter_and_the_Deathly_Hallows">Harry Potter and the Deathly Hallows</a> - J. K. Rowling - Late to the game, but I can now officially call myself a Rowling/Potter fanboy. The final book did not disappoint. </li>
<li><a href="https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/6396579-the-golden-mean">Golden Mean</a> - Annabel Lyon - Fictional account of Aristotle’s years tutoring Alexander (later to become The Great) of Macedon. A search for the mean between action and thought.</li>
<li><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Cat%27s_Table">The Cat’s Table</a> - Michael Ondaatje - Friendship!</li>
<li><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Purity_%28novel%29">Purity</a> - Jonathan Frazen - Although sunlight is a disinfectant, too much is a cancer.</li>
<li><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seveneves">Seveneves</a> - Neal Stephenson - We never learn who blew up the moon. Survival story. Post-apocalypse far-future history.</li>
<li><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Embassytown">Embassytown</a> - China Miéville - Must-read for language nerds. Aliens whose native language doesn’t support falsehoods. Through humans they learn to bridge similes into lies.</li>
<li><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A_Thousand_Splendid_Suns">A Thousand Splendid Suns</a> - Khaled Hosseini - The lives of girls and women in Afghanistan, 1960 to 2000.</li>
<li><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Children_of_Dune">Children of Dune</a> - Frank Herbert - Free will versus prophetic determinism on the formerly desert planet. </li>
<li><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Wise_Man%27s_Fear">The Wise Man’s Fear</a> - Patrick Rothfuss - Out-of-the-pan-into-the-fire hero fantasy of epic scale. Much fun.  </li>
</ul><p>Read in that order. No incompletes or duds this year.</p>

<p>It’s my usual mix of science fiction (How to live…, Seveneves, Embassytown, Children of Dune), fantasy (Harry Potter, The Wise Man’s Fear) and historical fiction (Q, Golden Mean), with a dash of “coming of age” (The Cat’s Table). Purity and A Thousand Splendid Suns sit outside my wheelhouse and I thank my sister for those. I found ten of the thirteen novels at Value Village. Still rocking the serendipity-driven reading plan.</p>

<h3>Top Three Fiction Reads in 2016</h3>

<p>Oh boy! Can I pick more than three? No?</p>

<p><strong><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seveneves">Seveneves</a> by Neal Stephenson</strong></p>

<blockquote>
  <p>“As it turned out, imagining the fate of seven billion people was far less emotionally affecting than imagining the fate of one.”</p>
</blockquote>

<p>The moon explodes. Within two years moon parts will rain down on earth, destroying the planet’s surface. 1,500 humans are selected to live in space, the other 7 billion will die. The only plan is to orbit earth and survive the 5000 year wait until the planet is re-habitable. The page count is worth it. Includes orbital-mechanics porn.</p>

<p><strong><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A_Thousand_Splendid_Suns">A Thousand Splendid Suns</a> by Khaled Hosseini</strong></p>

<p>Summarized in a quote and a poem:</p>

<blockquote>
  <p>“Learn this now and learn this well, my daughter: Like a compass needle that points north, a man’s accusing finger always finds a woman.”<br>
  - Khaled Hosseini</p>
  
  <p>“Every street of Kabul is enthralling to the eye<br>
  Through the bazaars, caravans of Egypt pass<br>
  One could not count the moons that shimmer on her roofs<br>
  And the thousand splendid suns that hide behind her walls”<br>
  - 17th-century Iranian poet Saib Tabriz</p>
</blockquote>

<p><strong><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Children_of_Dune">Children of Dune</a> by Frank Herbert</strong></p>

<blockquote>
  <p>“The malady of indifference is what destroys many things. Yes, even civilizations die of it. It’s as though that were the price demanded for achieving new levels of complexity or consciousness.”</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Where there is energy there is struggle. Abandon certainty! That’s life’s deepest command. The only order is the order we create ourselves. Fear is still the mind killer. :)</p>

<p><strong><a href="http://tim.blog/podcast/">The Tim Ferris Show</a></strong></p>

<p>Binge listened to over 150 episodes of the Tim Ferris Show in 2016. Most episodes are long-form (1 to 2 hour) interviews with interesting people. Tim has a knack for making his guest feel comfortable and chatty. I likely could have pick 50+ favourites, but here’s three.</p>

<p>Fav Episodes:</p>

<ul><li><a href="http://tim.blog/2015/07/28/jane-mcgonigal/">#93 - Jane McGonigal</a> - <a href="https://janemcgonigal.com/">Jane</a> is a world-renowned game designer and the Director of Games Research &amp; Development at the <a href="http://www.iftf.org">Institute for the Future</a>. Her research focuses on how games are transforming the way we lead our real lives, and how they can be used to increase our resilience and well-being. <a href="https://overcast.fm/+BmGV6rUt8">Listen on the web</a>.</li>
<li><a href="http://tim.blog/2016/05/04/mike-rowe/">#157 - Mike Rowe</a> - Mike’s performing career began in 1984 when he faked his way into the Baltimore Opera. His transition to television occurred in 1990 when — to settle a bet — he auditioned for the Shopping Channel and was hired after talking about a pencil for eight minutes. Amazing story teller. <a href="https://overcast.fm/+BmGU4SZtg/">Listen on the web</a>.</li>
<li><a href="http://tim.blog/2016/03/23/josh-waitzkin-the-prodigy-returns/">#148 - Josh Waitzkin</a> -  Josh was the basis for the book and movie Searching for Bobby Fischer. Considered a chess prodigy, he has perfected learning strategies that can be applied to anything, including his other loves of Brazilian jiu-jitsu (he’s a black belt under Marcelo Garcia) and Tai Chi push hands (he’s a world champion). He talks about everything from dynamic quality (Zen and the Art) to parenting to athletic training and learning. <a href="https://overcast.fm/+BmGUJyr8Q/">Listen on the web</a>.</li>
</ul><p><strong><a href="http://bigthink.com/articles?blog=think-again-podcast">Think Again</a></strong></p>

<p>I’ve listened to all 90 episodes starting in June 2015. Host Jason Gots and guests explore surprise topics.The conversations are fun and wide-ranging.</p>
